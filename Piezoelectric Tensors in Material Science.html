Piezoelectric Tensors in Material Science

Understanding the role of Tensorsin piezoelectric behavior

1. Piezoelectric Strain and Stress Relations

The core piezoelectric behavior is governed by two constitutive equations that describe how mechanical and electrical quantities interact:

Direct Piezoelectric Effect (Charge Generation)

\[ D_i = d_{ijk} T_{jk} + \varepsilon_{ij} E_j \]

  • Di: Electric displacement field (vector)
  • dijk: Piezoelectric strain tensor (third-order tensor)
  • Tjk: Stress tensor (second-order tensor)
  • Îµij: Permittivity tensor (second-order tensor)
  • Ej: Electric field (vector)

Inverse Piezoelectric Effect (Strain Generation)

\[ S_{ij} = s_{ijkl} T_{kl} + d_{kij} E_k \]

  • Sij: Strain tensor (second-order tensor)
  • sijkl: Elastic compliance tensor (fourth-order tensor)

2. Tensors in Material Properties

Tensors define various material properties:

  • Elasticity Tensor \(s_{ijkl}\) (4th order) relates stress and strain.
  • Piezoelectric Tensor \(d_{ijk}\) (3rd order) couples mechanical and electrical fields.
  • Dielectric Permittivity Tensor \(\varepsilon_{ij}\) (2nd order) describes how the material stores electrical energy.

3. Simplifications Using Voigt Notation

Instead of using full tensor notation, Voigt notation compresses indices for practical computations.

Example: \(d_{ijk}\) is written as a \(3 \times 6\) matrix instead of a full 3rd-order tensor.

4. Tensor Symmetries

- Elastic Tensor: Due to material symmetry, many components of \(s_{ijkl}\) are zero or equal.
- Piezoelectric Tensor: Limited by crystal symmetry; e.g., for a 6mm symmetry (PZT), only specific components like \(d_{31}\), \(d_{33}\), and \(d_{15}\) exist.

5. Finite Element Modeling (FEM)

Piezoelectric behavior in FEM uses tensorial relations to solve coupled electro-mechanical equations. Software like COMSOL, ANSYS, and Abaqus employ these tensor formulations to simulate piezoelectric devices.
